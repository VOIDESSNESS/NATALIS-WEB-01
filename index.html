THE CRISIS IN NUMBERS

Fifty years of decline.
No floor in sight.

The Total Fertility Rate — the average number of children per woman — has fallen continuously since 1963. The global average is 2.30. The replacement rate is 2.10. That gap seems small. What it produces over 50 years is civilisational.

THE ECONOMIC REALITY

The system was never built
for this.

Fertility decline is not primarily a cultural shift. It is the rational output of an economic system that has made parenthood financially catastrophic. Wages have not kept pace with housing, childcare, or education costs in any developed economy since the 1980s. The arithmetic of having a child simply does not work.

Every effective intervention in the data has one thing in common: it materially reduced the unit cost of raising a child. Not with cash. With structural change — housing supply, universal childcare, career protection. The governments that understood this are the only ones with TFRs above 1.8."
THE OTHER STORY

Africa:
The demographic future.

While the developed world empties, Africa grows. By 2100, Africa will hold nearly 4 billion people — more than the entire world in 1970. This is not simply good news. It is a different kind of challenge: whether population growth can be converted into demographic dividend before it becomes resource stress. The world's future workers will be African. The question is whether anyone is ready for that.

ON THE POINT OF NO RETURN
"Zero countries have ever recovered from a TFR below 1.5 sustained for more than a decade. This is not a data gap. It is a physical law of demographic systems: once a society internalises the small-family norm at scale, the economic and cultural feedback loops that produced it become self-sustaining.

The people who choose not to have children in high-cost environments are not broken. They are rational. And that is the most frightening sentence in this entire platform."
ON WHAT HAPPENS NEXT
"The optimistic scenario is not impossible. It requires structural reforms so profound they have never been accomplished in peacetime: housing below 5x income, universal childcare covering real working hours, paid leave that does not punish the parent who takes it.

The pessimistic scenario requires nothing. It requires exactly what is currently happening. No change. No intervention. Just the patient arithmetic accumulation of a system's consequences."
